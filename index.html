Why this exists

Most football analytics tutorials hand you a tidy CSV and skip straight to the charts. That hides the real work — and the real understanding. These notebooks do the full journey.

🔬

Raw event parsing

Every event carries type IDs, qualifier codes, and outcome flags. Decoding those — not using someone else's decoder — is where understanding comes from.

⚙️

Built-from-scratch metrics

🎨

Intentional visuals

The pipeline

Four stages, repeated for every notebook. Each stage produces something the next one depends on.

01

Scrape

Selenium renders each match page; raw JSON is pulled from the page state across a full season

02

Opta event type IDs, qualifier codes, and outcome flags are decoded into typed, filterable records

03

Engineer

Feature engineering

The metrics you care about in football aren't served up ready-made. Here's what gets built.

Carry reconstruction

Opta doesn't log ball carries as explicit events. They're inferred from consecutive possession events by the same player — checking time gap (<12 s), spatial gap (2.5–35 units), and directionality. Carry speed is distance ÷ dt, mapped to a colour scale.

Expected Threat (xT)

Karun Singh's 16×12 pitch grid assigns each zone a probability of leading to a goal. For every successful pass or cross, xT gained = xT(end_zone) − xT(start_zone). Zone totals accumulate across the season into a heatmap.

Open-play filtering

Set-piece actions (corners, free kicks, throw-ins, goal kicks) are stripped using Opta qualifier codes 5, 6, 107, and 124, so pass maps and chance-creation maps reflect genuine open-play patterns.

Post-action chaining

Checks whether a dribble or carry led to a shot within a fixed window — 3 team actions or 15 seconds — by walking forward through the sorted event stream. Flags the most dangerous sequences for the visual.

Off-ball run detection

Identifies receipt events in the final third where the gap since the player's last touch exceeds a threshold, suggesting a run into space rather than a stationary receipt.

Pre-action movement distance

For midfielders, estimates how far a player moved before receiving or before a defensive action — a proxy for off-ball engine work that doesn't show up in on-ball stats at all.

Notebooks

Three notebooks, each a step up in complexity — single player, two-player comparison, three-player comparison with a different position profile.

🎯

Single Forward Analysis + Chance Creation

One player · Full season · 11 visuals · Example: Yan Diomandé (RB Leipzig, 2025/26)

A complete solo breakdown of an attacking player — how they receive the ball, where they drive with it, how they create chances, and what their passing does to the defensive shape. Every visual is independent and readable on its own; together they build a full picture.

Outputs cover: touch density, off-ball runs, chance creation (open play only), take-on success/failure, post-dribble actions coloured by xT gained, progressive carry speed, xT zone map, final-third passing connections, foot split, crosses into the box, and a summary radar with stat block.

touch_map off_ball_runs chance_creation takeon_map xT_zones progressive_carries summary_radar
⚖️

Two Forward Comparison

Two players · Side-by-side · 10 visuals · Example: Victor Muñoz vs Luis Díaz

Takes the solo analysis and extends it to a direct comparison — both players rendered on mirrored pitches within each visual. The example is deliberately cross-league and cross-season (Osasuna 2025/26 vs Liverpool 2024/25) to show the pipeline handles different data prefixes cleanly and that the metrics are comparable even when the context differs.

side_by_side cross_season xT_comparison carry_speed foot_split
🧭

Three-Way Midfielder Comparison — Ligue 1

Bouaddi · Camaraet · Sangaré · 2025/26 · 10 visuals · 2-above-1 pitch layout

A midfielder-specific comparison with a richer defensive and transition metric set. The three-player layout moves to a 2-above-1 format to keep each pitch readable at smaller sizes. The added metrics — fast pass maps, ball recovery maps, post-recovery distribution, and pre-action movement — capture what central midfielders do that doesn't show up in chance-creation numbers.
